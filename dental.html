Has your practice ever completed a HIPAA Security Risk Analysis?

Most practices we ask answer no, or "I think our IT company did something once." Neither one holds up in an investigation.

The Security Risk Analysis has been federally required since 2005 under 45 CFR 164.308(a)(1)(ii)(A). In late 2024, the HHS Office for Civil Rights launched a dedicated enforcement campaign, the Risk Analysis Initiative, built entirely around that one requirement, and most of the resulting settlements have involved small and mid-sized providers, not hospital systems. It's the first document investigators request after any breach or complaint.

Questions dentists actually ask

Doesn't our IT company already handle this?

IT keeps things running. Compliance is a different job: a Security Risk Analysis is a specific federal document with specific required content, and "our IT guy set up the firewall" has never satisfied an investigator. We work alongside your IT provider, not against them.

One question worth asking them today: do you have a signed Business Associate Agreement with them? If they touch patient data and the answer is no, that's a finding all by itself.

We're a small practice. Would OCR really look at us?

Small practices are exactly who's being looked at. Most settlements under OCR's Risk Analysis Initiative have involved small and mid-sized providers, and in a recent year over half of all OCR settlements were imposed on small practices. Enforcement usually starts with something ordinary (a patient complaint, a stolen laptop, a phishing email), and the first request is always the same: show us your risk analysis.

How long does it take, and will it disrupt patients?

Two to three weeks start to finish, with one on-site visit, scheduled on your admin day, typically a Friday. Your patient schedule never changes.

What about the big new HIPAA security rules I've heard about?

A major overhaul of the Security Rule was proposed in early 2025, but it is not final; the target date has slipped to 2027 and it may still change. What matters today is the current rule, which is actively enforced right now. Practices that handle today's requirements well will be in the best position whenever new ones land.

What does a breach actually cost a practice like ours?

Recent OCR settlements with small providers have run from tens of thousands of dollars up, plus a multi-year corrective action plan, legal fees, patient notification costs, and your name on a public government website. Texas adds its own penalties on top, including fines for late notification. The risk analysis costs less than any single one of those line items.

Do you sell equipment or software?

No. We don't resell hardware, take commissions from vendors, or push a product line. When something needs to be purchased (a backup service, better licensing), we tell you what to buy and you buy it directly. Our only product is the work.
